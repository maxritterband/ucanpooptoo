Common questions

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers to the questions parents ask us most. This information is educational and not a substitute for your physician's advice.

Does encopresis ever stop?

Most people do eventually overcome encopresis, but the timeframe varies based on the individual and any other medical or behavioral issues they may be dealing with. What works for one child may not work for another, and some children have encopresis for longer periods.

If your child continues to struggle with accidents, it's important to seek help through a physician consultation.

Acknowledging encopresis & finding help

Many parents feel isolated, embarrassed, or frustrated about their child's encopresis and so don't seek help — but you shouldn't just wait for a child to “grow out of it.” According to the American Academy of Pediatrics, one of the most important tasks as a parent is to quickly seek a solution. If symptoms are allowed to continue, the child's self-esteem and social confidence can suffer. Consult your child's doctor or locate a pediatric gastroenterology specialist familiar with encopresis.

Our Tips for Parents section is a good place to start.

Should I take my child out of school? / Working with schools

Many children have more difficulty at school than at home, and parents often have questions about working with schools. Coordinate with teachers, school nurses, and other educators, and ask about accommodations such as a 504 plan.

Treatment also works best with a team approach — the child, parents, teachers, and health professionals working together on a plan that's both feasible and supported by research. Even with effective treatment, children generally take up to six months to fully recover, and relapses are common during times of change, so ongoing support and monitoring are crucial.

Why do we have relapses?

Overcoming encopresis is less a quick fix than a long-term commitment. Many children have initial success, then relapse into old habits. That's not failure — just a small step backwards. The important thing is not to wait, but to get back on track immediately and go step by step.

If your child was doing well but is now having frequent accidents again despite following the clean-out plan, consult your physician — they may be able to determine whether there's still an impaction.

Is this a behavioral problem?

In most cases, children with encopresis do not have accidents on purpose, out of spite, or because they are lazy. Rather, many secondary behavioral problems occur as a result of the encopresis. Once it's treated, many of those problems improve — but behavior therapy alone is usually not sufficient to cure it.

For more on the underlying mechanics, see Possible Causes.

Accidents at night

For children with a long history of encopresis, the muscles sometimes relax during sleep. This can happen if there's a lot of withholding during the day, or if the child has poor muscle control from longstanding constipation and encopresis. Discuss next steps with your child's physician, which may include a clean-out regimen and laxative maintenance therapy.

Handling encopresis across two homes

Consistency and communication are key. Involve the other parent or guardian, and get your child actively involved too. Children can and should play an active role in ending accidents: it helps when they understand the underlying causes, know how to poop correctly, and work on re-training their bodies to recognize when to go.

We only have accidents at home

One useful technique is charting the accidents in a diary. This can help you see whether they're happening at the same time of day and, together with your child's physician, make good decisions about the timing of interventions and medications. Charting is an important part of understanding and treating encopresis.
