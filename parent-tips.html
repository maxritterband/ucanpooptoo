For parents

Tips for Parents

Practical, real-world tips drawn from clinicians and from other parents. They aren't a substitute for your doctor's guidance, but they can help you support your child day to day.

Recognize that it's a real problem

Most parents first hope their child will simply outgrow encopresis, but it rarely resolves on its own. Recognizing it as a real medical problem — not laziness or defiance — is the first step toward treating it. The sooner you act, the better the outlook.

Get their legs supported

Do your child's legs reach the floor when they sit on the toilet? If not, place a footstool in front of the toilet so they can rest their legs and feet on a solid surface. Good support helps them relax and push effectively.

Check the sitting position

Make sure your child isn't tightly wrapping their legs around the base of the toilet. Their legs should rest loosely on the floor or a stool, which lets the muscles relax enough to pass a bowel movement.

Understand laxatives

Many parents — and even some physicians — worry about laxatives, but most of those concerns are based on myths, not facts. Most laxatives simply keep water in the stool to keep it soft, and the common ones used for encopresis are not addictive. Use them as directed by your child's doctor.

Clean out first, then maintain

Effective treatment usually starts with successfully emptying (“cleaning out”) the colon. If accidents continue, simply changing the daily laxative dose is typically not enough — talk with your physician about whether a clean-out is needed and whether there may still be an impaction.

Treat it as a team

Treatment works best as a team effort among the child, parents, teachers, and health professionals. Even with effective treatment, full recovery often takes up to six months, and relapses are common during times of change. As Dr. Coehlo puts it, “children with encopresis need understanding, support, and encouragement to be successful.”

Work with your child's school

Children often have more difficulty at school than at home. Coordinate with teachers and the school nurse so your child can use the bathroom when needed, and ask whether a 504 plan would help put supportive accommodations in place.

Plan ahead for trips

Travel and vacations disrupt the routines that keep things on track. Anticipate changes to schedule, diet, and bathroom access, and keep the maintenance plan going as much as possible while you're away.

Don't give up

Encopresis can be difficult to treat — even pediatricians and family doctors find it challenging. If you're feeling fed up, don't give up. Get back on track right away, go step by step, and consult a physician who understands encopresis.
