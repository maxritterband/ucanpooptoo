About encopresis

Possible Causes

In almost all cases, encopresis develops out of long-standing constipation. Here's what's actually happening inside.

Chronic constipation in children often leads to large, hard, difficult-to-pass bowel movements. As a result, the child may “hold back,” not completely emptying their colon when they poop, to avoid pain. Or during times of stress or limited access to a toilet, a child may “hold back.” Regardless of why, as the child holds back, they slowly fill up their colon with stool, stretching it out bigger and bigger. Doctors often call this megacolon, meaning the colon is larger than normal. This is why children with chronic constipation or encopresis can pass bowel movements that are extremely large — sometimes large enough to clog the toilet.

Since children with chronic constipation often hold back, they don't regularly empty the very bottom part of their colon (the rectum), allowing their colon to continue removing water from the waste and creating hard stools. This also means their rectum is often full. Normally, when we pass a bowel movement, we completely empty the colon, so most of the time the rectum is empty. Once a day, twice a day, or every other day, some stool moves into the empty rectum and stretches it. It is this stretching that gives us the feeling or urge to go — and we respond by going to the bathroom and emptying the rectum.

Because children with chronic constipation and encopresis almost always have some stool in their rectum, the nerves that send the signal to the brain are constantly being stimulated. Over time, those nerve sensations diminish, and the child's body ends up no longer recognizing the signal to poop. This isn't a conscious decision — it just happens. It's like living near a train station: at first you hear every train, but after a while you stop noticing. Children with chronic constipation and encopresis unconsciously tune out the normal signal to pass a bowel movement.

As the colon gets more and more stretched out of shape, soft or liquid stool from higher up begins to “leak” around the hard stool. This may start as small amounts that streak or stain the underwear — and most parents initially assume their child just isn't wiping well.

As the colon continues to stretch, the leakage increases until children begin having full accidents, passing whole bowel movements in their pants. Because this stool comes from higher up in the colon and hasn't been completely digested, the accidents can be very dark and sticky, smell very bad, and have to be scraped off the skin and clothing. And because it's leakage of soft stool, children often don't feel the accidents happening — they may not even notice the odor, because constant exposure desensitizes the brain's smelling centers. As a result, kids with encopresis, especially at school or in public, may need a caring adult to quietly let them know when an accident has happened.

So, what's the solution?

The good news: because encopresis grows out of a physical process, that process can be reversed with the right plan. Treatment generally starts by cleaning out the colon and then keeping stools soft and regular so the stretched colon can shrink back and the normal urge to go can return — usually with medical guidance and consistent daily habits.
