The basics

What is encopresis?

When children have encopresis, it means they cannot control their bowel movements and have poop accidents in their underwear. Sometimes people use the words “soiling” or “fecal incontinence” to mean the same thing.

Encopresis is a frustrating and difficult disorder for children and their families, and it can be hard to treat. In almost all cases it develops as a result of long-standing constipation — most children with encopresis have a history of constipation or passing very large, painful poops. Sometimes neither the child nor the parents recall the constipation, because it was so long ago.

In most cases, children do not have accidents on purpose, out of spite, or because they are lazy. Many secondary behavioral problems develop as a result of the encopresis, and once it's treated, many of those problems improve. Behavior therapy alone, though, is usually not enough to solve it.

“One of the most important tasks for parents of children with encopresis is to seek early care.”

— American Academy of Pediatrics

Encopresis is a true medical problem that requires intervention and help. Numerous scientific studies show the importance of pursuing both medical management and behavioral training as soon as possible. Because it's a developmental disorder, many parents understandably believe a child will simply “grow out of it” — but no maturing-out effect has been scientifically documented.

Why early care matters

The hidden costs of waiting

Lower self-esteem

Parents rate low self-esteem as the most important consequence, and affected children show more emotional and behavioral difficulties than their peers.

Bernard-Bonnin, Haley, Belanger & Nadeau, 1993

School & social impact

Children with encopresis are often taunted, bullied, and ostracized — leading to low self-esteem and decreased academic achievement.

Har & Croffie, 2010
~$40/week

Cost of missed work

The cost in missed work, transportation, and toileting supplies averages about $40 per week — not including doctor visits.

Ritterband & Thorndike, 2005
